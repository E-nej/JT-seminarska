## General

Slovenian (slovenščina) is a South Slavic language and the official language of Slovenia. It is one of the few living languages to retain the **dual grammatical number** alongside singular and plural — a feature inherited from Proto-Slavic that has vanished from almost all other modern Slavic languages. Slovenian word order is relatively free (SVO by default), but reordering elements shifts emphasis and can produce poetic effects:

> Včeraj sem šel domov.
>
> Yesterday AUX gone home
>
> I went home yesterday. (neutral)

> Domov sem šel včeraj.
>
> Home AUX gone yesterday
>
> It was yesterday that I went home. (emphasizes 'yesterday')

Slovenian is a **fusional** language: a single ending simultaneously encodes case, number, and gender on nouns, adjectives, and pronouns. As in other Slavic languages, there are no articles (no equivalent of English "a" or "the"). Definiteness can sometimes be conveyed through adjective form (definite vs. indefinite adjective endings on masculine nouns), word order, or context.

A distinctive feature of Slovenian phonology is **preglas**: after a "soft" stem (one ending in c, č, š, ž, or j), an ending's -o- becomes -e-. For example, the instrumental singular of *korak* ("step") is *korakom*, but for *stric* ("uncle") it is *stricem*.

When certain consonant clusters become difficult to pronounce at a word boundary — typically in the nominative singular or the genitive dual/plural — a **fill vowel** (usually *e*, written as schwa /ə/) is inserted before the final consonant(s). For example, *igra* ("game") has the genitive plural *iger*, not **igr*.

Consonant alternations inherited from Proto-Slavic affect spelling and pronunciation throughout the grammar:

| Normal              | b   | p   | v   | m   | d | t  | s  | z  | g  | k  | h  |
|---------------------|-----|-----|-----|-----|---|----|----|----|----|----|-----|
| First palatalization | b   | p   | v   | m   | d | t  | s  | z  | **ž** | **č** | **š** |
| Second palatalization| b   | p   | v   | m   | d | t  | s  | z  | **z** | **c** | **s** |
| Iotation            | blj | plj | vlj | mlj | **j** | **č** | **š** | **ž** | **ž** | **č** | **š** |

The first palatalization appears in the present tense of consonant-stem verbs and before suffixes beginning with *e* or *i*. The second palatalization appears most often in the imperative. Iotation occurs in the present forms of certain *-ati* verbs.

## Nouns

### Gender

Slovenian nouns belong to one of three **genders**: masculine (moški spol), feminine (ženski spol), or neuter (srednji spol). Gender is usually predictable from the nominative singular ending:

- **Masculine**: most end in a consonant — *stol* (chair), *telefon* (phone), *pes* (dog). Some masculine nouns end in *-a* (e.g., *vodja* — leader, *Luka* — a male name) or are borrowed words ending in *-o* or *-i* (e.g., *avto* — car, *taksi* — taxi).
- **Feminine**: most end in *-a* — *miza* (table), *ženska* (woman), *reka* (river). A significant group of feminine nouns ends in a consonant; these are mostly i-stem nouns — *kost* (bone), *misel* (thought), *zanimivost* (interest), and nouns ending in *-ev* like *cerkev* (church) or *breskev* (peach).
- **Neuter**: nearly all end in *-o* or *-e* — *okno* (window), *morje* (sea), *mesto* (city), *drevo* (tree).

When in doubt, placing the demonstrative pronoun *tisti* ("that") in front of a noun is the native speakers' method for identifying gender:
- *tisti stol* → masculine
- *tista miza* → feminine
- *tisto okno* → neuter

### Number

Uniquely among modern European languages in widespread use, Slovenian retains **three grammatical numbers**:

1. **Singular (ednina)** — one of something: *miza* (a/the table)
2. **Dual (dvojina)** — exactly two of something: *mizi* (two tables)
3. **Plural (množina)** — three or more: *mize* (tables)

The dual is used consistently in standard Slovenian and carries real communicative weight. Compare:

> Odšla sva v park. Usedla sva se na klopco in se pogovarjala.
>
> We-two went to the park. We-two sat on a bench and talked.

> Odšli smo v park. Usedli smo se na klopco in se pogovarjali.
>
> We (all) went to the park. We sat on a bench and talked.

The first version sounds noticeably more intimate and romantic to a Slovenian ear — a nuance untranslatable into English. Note that for natural pairs (eyes, ears, trousers), the plural is used rather than the dual.

### Case

Slovenian has **six grammatical cases** (skloni), each answering a specific set of questions and governing specific prepositions:

| # | Case | Slovenian | Question words | Core usage |
|---|------|-----------|----------------|------------|
| 1 | Nominative | Imenovalnik | Kdo? Kaj? (Who? What?) | Subject of a sentence |
| 2 | Genitive | Rodilnik | Koga? Česa? (Of whom? Of what?) | Possession, negation, partitive, certain prepositions |
| 3 | Dative | Dajalnik | Komu? Čemu? (To whom? To what?) | Indirect object |
| 4 | Accusative | Tožilnik | Koga? Kaj? (Whom? What?) | Direct object, direction |
| 5 | Locative | Mestnik | O kom? O čem? (About whom? About what?) | Location (always with a preposition), topic |
| 6 | Instrumental | Orodnik | S kom? S čim? (With whom? With what?) | Means, accompaniment (always with a preposition) |

A historical seventh case, the **vocative**, existed until the 18th century. Traces survive in a few borrowings from Serbo-Croatian (e.g., *bog* → *bože*), but in modern standard Slovenian the nominative serves as the address form.

Genitive is used after negation (a key rule):
> Vidim zvezde. — I see the stars. (accusative)
> Ne vidim zvezd. — I don't see the stars. (genitive)

Case usage examples:

| Case | Example | Translation |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Nominative | Moj stol je v sobi. | My chair is in the room. |
| Genitive | Mojega stola ni v sobi. | My chair is not in the room. |
| Genitive | Košček papirja mi je ostal v dlani. | A piece of paper remained in my palm. |
| Dative | Beraču je dal denar. | He gave money to a beggar. |
| Accusative | Vidim zvezde. | I see the stars. |
| Locative | Mnogo je rečenega o novem sodniku. | A lot is being said about the new judge. |
| Instrumental | Na sprehod grem s svojim psom. | I am going for a walk with my dog. |

### Declension Patterns

Slovenian has **10 declension types** across the three genders (four per gender, though the fourth is always adjectival). The declension type is traditionally identified by the **genitive singular ending**.

**Masculine noun declensions:**

1. **First masculine (o-stem)** — genitive singular in *-a* or *-u*; e.g., *napor* (effort), *pes* (dog), *grad* (castle). This is by far the most common. Animate nouns (referring to persons or animals) have their accusative singular equal to the genitive; inanimate nouns have accusative equal to nominative.
2. **Second masculine (a-stem)** — genitive singular in *-e*; e.g., *vodja* (leader), *vojvoda* (duke), *Luka*.
3. **Third masculine (no endings)** — does not take endings in any case; e.g., borrowed words like *atelje*, *finale*.
4. **Fourth masculine (adjectival)** — genitive singular in *-ega*; nominalized adjectives like *moški* (man, literally "male one"), *dežurni* (the duty officer).

Example declension of *brat* (brother) — first masculine, hard stem:

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|------|----------|------|--------|
| Nominative | brat | brata | bratje |
| Genitive | brata | bratov | bratov |
| Dative | bratu | bratoma | bratom |
| Accusative | brata | brata | brate |
| Locative | bratu | bratih | bratih |
| Instrumental | z bratom | z bratoma | z brati |

**Feminine noun declensions:**

1. **First feminine (a-stem)** — genitive singular in *-e*, genitive plural not in *-ih*; e.g., *žena* (woman), *lipa* (linden tree), *cerkev* (church — former v-stem).
2. **Second feminine (i-stem)** — genitive singular in *-i*; e.g., *kost* (bone), *luč* (light), *zanimivost* (interest), *misel* (thought).
3. **Third feminine (no endings)** — no endings in any case; e.g., *mami* (mommy), foreign names like *Karin*.
4. **Fourth feminine (adjectival)** — genitive singular in *-e*, genitive plural in *-ih*; nominalized adjectives like *dežurna* (duty officer, fem.).

Example declension of *žena* (woman) — first feminine:

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|------|----------|------|--------|
| Nominative | žena | ženi | žene |
| Genitive | žene | žena | žena |
| Dative | ženi | ženama | ženam |
| Accusative | ženo | ženi | žene |
| Locative | ženi | ženah | ženah |
| Instrumental | z ženo | z ženama | z ženami |

**Neuter noun declensions:**

1. **First neuter (o-stem)** — genitive singular in *-a*; the vast majority of neuter nouns; e.g., *mesto* (city), *drevo* (tree), *breme* (burden), *dekle* (girl), *poslopje* (building).
2. **Second neuter (a-stem)** — only personal pronouns follow this.
3. **Third neuter** and **fourth neuter** cover further sub-patterns and historical n-, s-, t-stems.

Example declension of *mesto* (city) — first neuter:

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|------|----------|------|--------|
| Nominative | mesto | mesti | mesta |
| Genitive | mesta | mest | mest |
| Dative | mestu | mestoma | mestom |
| Accusative | mesto | mesti | mesta |
| Locative | mestu | mestih | mestih |
| Instrumental | z mestom | z mestoma | z mesti |

### Special and Irregular Nouns

Some high-frequency nouns have irregular or split declension patterns that must be memorized:

- **dan** (day, masc.) — the stem alternates: *dan* → *dneva* (gen. sg.) or keeps the short form. Both the short and long forms are correct in most cases.
- **človek** (person/human, masc.) — singular and plural are radically different: singular *človek*, but plural *ljudje* (people), with its own plural endings.
- **otrok** (child, masc.) — mostly regular but with a small deviation in some forms.
- **hči** (daughter, fem.) — the stem expands: *hči* → *hčere* (gen. sg.).
- **mati** (mother, fem.) — does not end in *-a* yet is feminine; *mati* → *matere* (gen. sg.). (*Mami*, "mommy", is never declined at all.)
- **oko** (eye, neut. sg.) vs. **oči** (eyes, fem. pl.) — the singular and the plural belong to different genders and different declensions.
- **tla** (floor/floors) — neuter, exists only in the plural.

Some nouns are **homographs** with different genders and meanings, requiring different declensions:
- *kap* (masc.) = gutter vs. *kap* (fem.) = stroke
- *klop* (fem.) = bench vs. *klop* (masc.) = tick
- *pot* (fem.) = path vs. *pot* (masc.) = sweat
- *prst* (masc.) = finger vs. *prst* (fem.) = soft soil

## Adjectives

### Types

Slovenian distinguishes three main types of adjective:

1. **Qualitative (kakovostni)** — express properties or qualities: *lep* (beautiful), *hiter* (fast), *bolan* (ill). These are gradable.
2. **Relational (vrstni)** — express type, class, material, or category: *kemijska sprememba* (chemical change), *fotografski aparat* (photographic device/camera). Generally not gradable.
3. **Possessive (svojilni)** — express ownership or origin: *bratov avto* (brother's car), *Karmenina torbica* (Karmen's handbag), *delavska halja* (workers' overall).

### Definite and Indefinite Forms

Masculine singular adjectives in the nominative have **two forms** indicating specificity:

- **Definite (določna oblika)** — ending *-ni* or *-i*; the noun is already known or of a specific type. Question word: *Kateri?* (Which?)
  > Stari učitelj je to dejal. — The old teacher said this. (implying there is another, younger teacher)

- **Indefinite (nedoločna oblika)** — ending *-en* or no ending; the noun is unspecified, mentioned for the first time, or generic. Question word: *Kakšen?* (What sort of?)
  > Star učitelj je to dejal. — An old teacher said this.

This distinction only applies to masculine singular nominative (and sometimes accusative). Feminine and neuter, and all other cases and numbers, use a single adjectival form. Adjectives ending in *-i* (like *slovenski*, *kmečki*, relational adjectives) and all possessive adjectives exist only in the definite form.

Two adjectives have separate definite and indefinite forms for all genders and all cases: **majhen/mali** (small) and **velik/veliki** (big).

### Agreement

Adjectives agree with the noun they modify in **gender, number, and case**. If an adjective modifies two singular nouns together, it goes into the dual; if it modifies three or more, the plural:

> Mesto in vas sta bila proti predlogu občine.
> The city (neut.) and the village (fem.) were-DUAL against the municipality's proposal.

Some adjectives are never declined, mostly loanwords: *bež* (beige), *poceni* (cheap), *roza* (pink), *super*, *seksi*.

### Possessive Adjectives

Possessive adjectives are formed by adding a suffix to the possessor noun:
- **-ov** (or **-ev** after soft stems: c, č, ž, š, j) for masculine and neuter possessed nouns: *brat* → *bratov* (brother's), *stric* → *stričev* (uncle's)
- **-in** for feminine possessed nouns: *sestra* → *sestrin* (sister's), *mama* → *mamin* (mom's)

When derived from proper names, these are capitalized: *Marijn* (Marija's), *Tonetov* (Tone's).

### Negative Adjectives

Negative adjectives are formed with the prefix **ne-**:
- *lep* → *nelep* (beautiful → not beautiful, though not necessarily ugly)
- *moralen* → *nemoralen* (moral → immoral)
- *legitimen* → *nelegitimen* (legitimate → illegitimate)
- *reverzibilen* → *nereverzibilen* (reversible → irreversible)

### Comparison

The **comparative** is formed in one of four ways:
1. Adding the ending **-ši / -ša / -še**: *lep* → *lepši* (beautiful → more beautiful), *trd* → *trši* (hard → harder, with *-d-* dropping out)
2. Adding the ending **-ejši / -ejša / -ejše**: *zanimiv* → *zanimivejši* (interesting → more interesting), *transparenten* → *transparentnejši* (transparent → more transparent, with *-e-* dropping out)
3. Adding the ending **-ji / -ja / -je** (with stem changes): *globok* → *globlji* (deep → deeper; note the added *-l-* and loss of *-o-* and *-k-*)
4. Using the word **bolj** (more) before the adjective — required for colors and adjectives whose stems don't lend themselves to a suffix: *bolj zelen* (greener), *bolj otročji* (more childish)

The **superlative** is formed by prepending **naj-** directly before the comparative form (whether one word or two):
- *lep* → *lepši* → **najlepši**
- *zelen* → *bolj zelen* → **najbolj zelen**
- *zanimiv* → *zanimivejši* → **najzanimivejši** (though *najbolj zanimiv* is more common in speech)
- *globok* → *globlji* → **najgloblji**

## Pronouns

### Personal Pronouns

Personal pronouns in Slovenian are declined in all six cases and in three numbers, reflecting the dual. Many pronouns have both a **full (stressed) form** and a **clitic (unstressed, shortened) form** that attaches to adjacent words.

The nominative (subject) forms are typically omitted in neutral sentences because the verb ending already encodes person, number, and sometimes gender. The nominative pronoun is used only for **emphasis or contrast**:

> Jaz mislim drugače. — I (in particular) think otherwise.

**First person:**

| Case | Singular (jaz) | Dual (midva/midve) | Plural (mi/me) |
|------|---------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Nominative | jaz | midva (m) / midve (f) | mi (m) / me (f) |
| Genitive | mene / me | naju | nas |
| Dative | meni / mi | nama | nam |
| Accusative | mene / me | naju | nas |
| Locative | meni | nama | nas |
| Instrumental | mano | nama | nami |

**Second person:**

| Case | Singular (ti) | Dual (vidva/vidve) | Plural (vi/ve) |
|------|--------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Nominative | ti | vidva (m) / vidve (f) | vi (m) / ve (f) |
| Genitive | tebe / te | vaju | vas |
| Dative | tebi / ti | vama | vam |
| Accusative | tebe / te | vaju | vas |
| Locative | tebi | vama | vas |
| Instrumental | tabo | vama | vami |

**Third person:**

| Case | Sg. masc. (on) | Sg. fem. (ona) | Sg. neut. (ono) | Du. (onadva/onidve) | Pl. masc. (oni) | Pl. fem. (one) |
|------|---------------|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Nominative | on | ona | ono | onadva / onidve | oni | one |
| Genitive | njega / ga | nje / je | njega / ga | njiju | njih / jih | njih / jih |
| Dative | njemu / mu | njej / ji | njemu / mu | njima | njim / jim | njim / jim |
| Accusative | njega / ga | njo / jo | njega / ga | njiju | njih / jih | njih / jih |
| Locative | njem | njej | njem | njiju | njih | njih |
| Instrumental | z njim | z njo | z njim | z njima | z njimi | z njimi |

The clitic (short) forms cannot appear at the beginning of a sentence or after a preposition. After a preposition, the full forms are obligatory:

> Zanj mi je dal denar. — He gave me money for him. (clitic *ga* → *nj* after *za*)
> Za njega mi je dal denar. — He gave me money for him (in particular). (stressed)

### Reflexive Pronoun

The reflexive pronoun (*se / si*) refers back to the subject of the sentence regardless of person or number:

> Umivam **se**. — I am washing myself.
> Umivate **se**. — You are washing yourselves.
> Umivam **si** roke. — I am washing my hands.
> Sebi gradi grobnico. — He/She is building a tomb for him-/herself.
> S sabo/seboj ni zadovoljna. — She is not happy with herself.

The reciprocal meaning ("each other") can be expressed with *drug drugega*:
> Umivata **drug drugega**. — The two of them are washing each other.

### Interrogative Pronouns

There are two nominative interrogative pronouns: **kdo** (who) and **kaj** (what). They decline in all six cases:

> Kaj je ta stvar, ki se premika? — What is this thing that is moving?
> Komu naj dam to? — To whom ought I to give this?
> Česa ne smem storiti? — What may I not do?
> Vprašal sem ga, o kom je bil govoril. — I asked him about whom he had been talking.

### Relative Pronouns

The substantival relative pronoun is formed from the interrogative by adding **-r**: **kdor** (who, that; for persons), **kar** (which, that; for things):

> Kdor krade, ni pošten. — Someone who steals is not honest.
> Kar poveš, tega ne moreš več obvladovati. — Something that you say, you cannot control any more.

### Negative Pronouns

Negative pronouns are formed from the interrogative with the prefix **ni-**: **nihče** (nobody), **nič** (nothing). A negative pronoun **requires a negative predicate** — double negation is grammatically correct and obligatory in Slovenian:

> Nihče me nikoli ni maral. — Nobody ever liked me.
> Nikjer ni nikogar. — There is no one anywhere.
> Nič ni resnično. — Nothing is real.
> Od nikogar ne želim ničesar. — I don't want anything from anyone. (literally: I want nothing from no one)

### Indefinite Pronouns

Indefinite pronouns begin with **ne-**: **nekdo** (someone), **nekaj** (something). They decline like *kdo* and *kaj*:

> Nekoga so videli stati ob oknu. — They saw someone standing near the window.
> Zgodilo se je nekaj strašnega! — Something horrible has happened!

The relative-indefinite forms **kdorkoli / kdor koli** (whoever) and **karkoli / kar koli** (whatever) add *-koli* to the relative pronoun:

> Kdorkoli pokliče 112, mora znati povedati, kaj je narobe. — Whoever rings 112 must know how to say what is wrong.
> Karkoli stori, stori to dobro. — Whatever he/she does, he/she does it well.

### Possessive Pronouns

| Person | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| 1st | mój (my) | nájin (our two's) | nàš (our) |
| 2nd | tvój (your) | vájin (your two's) | vàš (your) |
| 3rd masc. | njegôv (his) | njún (their two's) | njíhov (their) |
| 3rd fem. | njén (her) | njún | njíhov |
| 3rd neut. | njegôv (its) | njún | njíhov |
| Reflexive | svój (one's own) | — | — |

The reflexive possessive **svoj** is used when the possessor is the subject of the clause — making many otherwise ambiguous English sentences unambiguous in Slovenian:

> Njeno brisačo je vzela v kopalnico. — She took her (= someone else's) towel into the bathroom.
> Svojo brisačo je vzela v kopalnico. — She took her (= her own) towel into the bathroom.

## Verbs

### Introduction

Slovenian verbs are conjugated for **person** (1st, 2nd, 3rd), **number** (singular, dual, plural), **tense** (present, past, pluperfect, future), **mood** (indicative, imperative, conditional), and **voice** (active, passive, reflexive). In the past tense, the participle also agrees in **gender**.

Verbs are given in the **infinitive** form (ending in *-ti* or *-či*), and the **1st person singular present tense** determines the conjugation pattern of all other present/imperative forms.

The infinitive and the **supine** are the two verbal nouns. The supine is used after verbs of motion to express purpose:
> Grem spat. — I am going to sleep. (supine of *spati*)
> Grem spati. — I am going to sleep. (infinitive — both forms are accepted in standard language)

### Verbal Aspect

As in all Slavic languages, Slovenian verbs come in **aspectual pairs**:

- **Imperfective (nedovršni)** — an ongoing, repeated, or habitual action
- **Perfective (dovršni)** — a completed, bounded action

Most verbs exist in matched pairs expressing the same concept with different aspects. Perfective verbs are often formed from imperfective verbs by adding a prefix:

| Imperfective | Perfective | Meaning |
|--------------|------------|---------|
| *pisati* | *napisati* | to write |
| *brati* | *prebrati* | to read |
| *delati* | *narediti* | to do / make |
| *skákati* | *skočíti* | to jump (continuously / once) |
| *gledati* | *pogledati* | to watch / to take a look |

Aspect affects meaning in crucial ways:
- In the **present tense**, an imperfective verb expresses a current or ongoing action; a perfective verb in the present tense expresses a future completed action:
  > *Pišem pismo.* — I am writing a letter. (imperfective, now)
  > *Napišem pismo.* — I will write a letter. (perfective, completed future action)

- In the **past tense**, aspect signals whether the action was completed:
  > *Bral sem knjigo.* — I was reading the book. (imperfective, possibly unfinished)
  > *Prebral sem knjigo.* — I read the book (and finished it). (perfective)

Imperfective verbs generally lack a **past passive participle**. Perfective verbs usually have no **present active participle**.

### Conjugation Classes

Verbs are traditionally grouped into conjugation classes based on the infinitive ending and the present tense stem:

1. **-ati verbs (a-type)**: present tense uses the vowel *-a-* before endings — *gledati* (to watch): *gledam, gledaš, gleda, gledava, gledata, gledata, gledamo, gledate, gledajo*
2. **-iti / -eti verbs (i-type)**: present uses *-i-* — *govoriti* (to speak): *govorim, govoriš, govori, govoriva, govorita, govorita, govorimo, govorite, govorijo*
3. **-ovati / -evati verbs**: the stem changes with *-uje-* in the present — *kupovati* (to buy): *kupujem, kupuješ, kupuje,* etc.
4. **Consonant-stem / irregular verbs**: stems ending in a consonant, requiring palatalization in certain forms — *peči* (to bake): *pečem, pečeš,* etc.

Note: the 3rd person singular present has **no ending** (zero ending). It is simply the bare vowel-stem: *gleda* (he/she watches), *govori* (he/she speaks).

### Present Tense (sedanjik)

Present tense endings for the three numbers:

| Person | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| 1st | **-m** | **-va** | **-mo** |
| 2nd | **-š** | **-ta** | **-te** |
| 3rd | — (zero) | **-ta** | **-jo / -ejo** |

Example — *imeti* (to have):

| Person | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| 1st | imam | imava | imamo |
| 2nd | imaš | imata | imate |
| 3rd | ima | imata | imajo |

Example — *govoriti* (to speak):

| Person | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| 1st | govorim | govoriva | govorimo |
| 2nd | govoriš | govorita | govorite |
| 3rd | govori | govorita | govorijo |

### Past Tense (preteklik)

The past tense (preterite) is **analytic**: formed with the **present tense of *biti* (to be)** + the **l-participle** (past active participle) of the main verb. The l-participle agrees with the subject in gender and number:

| Gender | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| Masculine | -l | -la | -li |
| Feminine | -la | -li | -le |
| Neuter | -lo | -li | -la |

Forms of *biti* in the present (used as past auxiliary):
*sem, si, je* (sg.) / *sva, sta, sta* (du.) / *smo, ste, so* (pl.)

Example — *govoriti* (to speak), male speaker:

| Person | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| 1st | govoril sem | govorila sva | govorili smo |
| 2nd | govoril si | govorila sta | govorili ste |
| 3rd | govoril je | govorila sta | govorili so |

Female speaker, 1st singular: *govorila sem*. Neuter/mixed group plural: *govorila so*.

The auxiliary verb is typically placed after the participle unless the participle is the first word, in which case the clitic auxiliary is placed in the **second position** (Wackernagel's Law):

> *Govoril sem.* — I spoke.
> *Včeraj sem govoril.* — Yesterday I spoke. (auxiliary after first word *včeraj*)

Standard Slovenian **no longer uses the aorist or imperfect** — these ancient Slavic tenses survive only in the Resian dialect and are fading even there. The preterite covers all past meanings.

### Pluperfect (predpreteklik)

The pluperfect expresses an action completed before another past action. It is formed with the **l-participle of *biti*** + the **l-participle of the main verb**:

> Bil sem govoril. — I had spoken.
> Ko je prišel, sem bil že odšel. — When he arrived, I had already left.

### Future Tense (prihodnjik)

The future tense is formed with the **future forms of *biti*** + the **l-participle** of the main verb:

Future forms of *biti*: *bom, boš, bo* (sg.) / *bova, bosta, bosta* (du.) / *bomo, boste, bodo* (pl.)

Example — *videti* (to see), masculine:

| Person | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| 1st | bom videl | bova videla | bomo videli |
| 2nd | boš videl | bosta videla | boste videli |
| 3rd | bo videl | bosta videla | bodo videli |

Only the verbs ***biti*** (to be) and ***iti*** (to go) have synthetic (single-word) future forms. For *biti*, the synthetic future is the only standard form:
*bom, boš, bo, bova, bosta, bosta, bomo, boste, bodo*

Perfective verbs inherently express completeness and so the present tense form of a perfective verb effectively functions as a future:
> *Napišem pismo.* — I will write the letter (and finish it).

### Imperative Mood (velelnik)

The imperative is used for commands or requests. It is formed in the **present tense**, primarily for the 2nd person singular and plural, with the 1st person dual and plural used for cohortative ("let's") meanings.

**Formation**: Remove the 3rd person plural present ending (*-jo*/*-ejo*/*-ijo*) and add:

| Person | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| 1st | — | **-iva** | **-imo** |
| 2nd | **-i** (or **-j** after a vowel) | **-ita** | **-ite** |

Examples:

| Infinitive | 2sg | 2du | 2pl | Meaning |
|------------|-----|-----|-----|---------|
| *brati* (to read) | *beri!* | *berita!* | *berite!* | Read! |
| *pisati* (to write) | *piši!* | *pišita!* | *pišite!* | Write! |
| *govoriti* (to speak) | *govori!* | *govorita!* | *govorite!* | Speak! |
| *iti* (to go) | *pojdi!* | *pojdita!* | *pojdite!* | Go! |
| *vzeti* (to take) | *vzemi!* | *vzemita!* | *vzemite!* | Take! |
| *dati* (to give) | *daj!* | *dajta!* | *dajte!* | Give! |

Negation of the imperative uses *ne* before the verb:
> *Ne govori!* — Don't speak!
> *Ne hodite sem!* — Don't come here!

### Conditional Mood (pogojnik)

The conditional is used for hypothetical situations ("would"). It is formed with the **particle *bi*** (invariable — same for all persons, genders, and numbers) + the **l-participle** of the main verb:

> Govoril bi. — He would speak.
> Govorila bi. — She would speak.
> Govorili bi. — They would speak.

The conditional of *biti* (to be) uses the same auxiliary *bi*:
> Bil bi doma. — He would be at home.

In a negative conditional, negation falls on *bi*: *ne bi* or *né bi*:
> Ne bi šel tja. — I would not go there.

Common uses:
> Rad bi šel. — I would like to go. (male speaker)
> Rada bi šla. — I would like to go. (female speaker)
> Če bi imel čas, bi prišel. — If I had the time, I would come.

### Voice

Slovenian has three voices:

1. **Active (tvorni način)**: the subject performs the action.
   > Jaz pišem pismo. — I write a letter.

2. **Passive (trpni način)**: the subject receives the action. Formed with *biti* + the **n-participle** (past passive participle, ending in *-n* or *-t*):
   > Pismo je pisano. — The letter is being written.
   > Pismo je bilo napisano. — The letter was written.
   Standard Slovenian prefers active voice and uses passive significantly less than English.

3. **Reflexive (refleksivni način)**: marked by the reflexive particle **se** (acc.) or **si** (dat.). All reflexive verbs are intransitive. The reflexive *se* can also express a passive-like meaning with certain verbs:
   > Umivam se. — I am washing (myself).
   > Tukaj se govori slovensko. — Slovenian is spoken here. (lit. "Here one speaks Slovenian")

### Participles

Slovenian verbs have four participles:

| Participle | Ending | Usage |
|------------|--------|-------|
| **Present active (č-participle)** | *-č / -joč / -eč* | Adjectival or adverbial; e.g., *pišoč* (writing), *govoreč* (speaking) |
| **Past active (l-participle)** | *-l / -la / -lo* | Core of past/future/conditional tenses; agrees in gender and number |
| **Past passive (n-participle)** | *-n / -na / -no* or *-t / -ta / -to* | Passive voice; e.g., *napisan* (written), *odprt* (opened) |
| **Present transgressive** | *-č* | Somewhat archaic in speech; found in literary language |

### Gerund (verbal noun)

A **gerund** (glagolnik) is a noun derived from a verb, denoting an action or state. It ends in **-anje** or **-enje** and is grammatically neuter:

| Verb | Gerund | Meaning |
|------|--------|---------|
| *pisati* (to write) | *pisanje* | writing |
| *goreti* (to burn) | *gorenje* | burning |
| *usklajevati* (to harmonize) | *usklajevanje* | harmonizing |
| *brenčati* (to buzz) | *brenčanje* | buzzing |
| *saditi* (to plant) | *sajenje* | planting |

> Pisanje ni naravno: potrebno se ga je priučiti. — Writing is not natural: it must be learnt.
> Brenčanje mrčesa me spravlja ob živce! — The buzzing of insects is driving me crazy!

### The Verb *biti* (to be)

*Biti* is the most important and irregular verb in Slovenian, serving as the main copula and as the auxiliary for all compound tenses.

Present tense of *biti*:

| Person | Affirmative | Negative |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| 1sg | sem | nisem |
| 2sg | si | nisi |
| 3sg | je | ni |
| 1du | sva | nisva |
| 2du | sta | nista |
| 3du | sta | nista |
| 1pl | smo | nismo |
| 2pl | ste | niste |
| 3pl | so | niso |

Future: *bom, boš, bo, bova, bosta, bosta, bomo, boste, bodo*
Past: *bil/bila/bilo sem* (I was)
Conditional auxiliary: *bi* (invariable)

### The Verb *iti* (to go)

*Iti* is another highly irregular verb, important for motion:

| Person | Present | Imperative |
|--------|---------|------------|
| 1sg | grem | — |
| 2sg | greš | pojdi! |
| 3sg | gre | — |
| 1du | greva | pojdiva! |
| 2du | gresta | pojdita! |
| 3du | gresta | — |
| 1pl | gremo | pojdimo! |
| 2pl | greste | pojdite! |
| 3pl | grejo | — |

Past: *šel sem* (I went, masc.), *šla sem* (fem.)
Future: *bom šel / šla*

### Modal Verbs

Modal verbs express ability, possibility, necessity, or will. They are usually followed by an infinitive:

| Modal verb | Present 1sg | Meaning |
|------------|------------|---------|
| *moči* | *morem* | to be able to, can |
| *morati* | *moram* | must, to have to |
| *smeti* | *smem* | may, to be allowed to |
| *hoteti* | *hočem* | to want, will |
| *znati* | *znam* | to know how to, can |

> Ne morem priti. — I cannot come.
> Moraš jesti. — You must eat.
> Smeš iti. — You may go.
> Hočem videti film. — I want to see the film.

## Adverbs

The adverb in Slovenian is always identical to the **singular neuter form** of any adjective derived from an adjective:

> *Dan je bil lep.* — The day was nice. (masculine adjective *lep*)
> *Bilo je lepo.* — It was nice. (neuter adjective = adverb *lepo*)
> *Govorili so lepo.* — They spoke nicely. (adverb *lepo*)

Adverbs can also be derived from:
- **Nouns**: *doma* (at home), *jeseni* (in autumn), *pozimi* (in winter)
- **Prepositional constructions**: *naglas* (aloud), *potem* (then), *skupaj* (together)
- **Verbs**: *nevede* (unknowingly), *skrivoma* (secretly), *mimogrede* (by the way)
- **Numerals**: *enkrat* (once), *dvakrat* (twice), *trikrat* (three times)

There are four main **semantic types** of adverb:

1. **Time (prislovni določili časa)**: *danes* (today), *včeraj* (yesterday), *jutri* (tomorrow), *zdaj* (now), *potem* (then), *večno* (perpetually), *vedno* (always), *nikoli* (never)
2. **Place (prislovni določili kraja)**: *tukaj* (here), *tam* (there), *doma* (at home), *domov* (homewards), *zunaj* (outside), *znotraj* (inside), *spodaj* (below), *zgoraj* (above)
3. **Manner (prislovni določili načina)**: *hitro* (quickly), *počasi* (slowly), *grdo* (uglily), *lepo* (nicely), *povsem* (entirely), *skupaj* (together)
4. **Cause/Reason (prislovni določili vzroka)**: *nalašč* (on purpose), *zato* (therefore), *zakaj* (why)

Adverbs are **gradable**, just like adjectives:

> To je storil natančno. — He did this carefully.
> Naslednjič pa še natančneje. — The next time, more carefully still.
> Najnatančneje od vseh je delal Janez. — Janez worked most carefully of all.

Some common Slovenian adverbs:

| Slovenian | English | Slovenian | English |
|-----------|---------|-----------|---------|
| danes | today | vedno | always |
| včeraj | yesterday | nikoli | never |
| jutri | tomorrow | že | already |
| zdaj / sedaj | now | še | still, yet |
| potem | then | kmalu | soon |
| takoj | immediately | pozno | late |
| skupaj | together | naenkrat | suddenly |
| zelo | very | precej | quite |
| malo | a little | preveč | too much |
| skoraj | almost | ravno | exactly, just |
| morda | perhaps | seveda | of course |
| verjetno | probably | res | really |
| tukaj | here | tam | there |
| povsod | everywhere | nikjer | nowhere |

## Prepositions

Slovenian prepositions govern specific cases. The same preposition can sometimes govern different cases with different meanings:

**Prepositions governing the Genitive:**
*iz* (from, out of), *brez* (without), *do* (to, until), *od* (from, since, of), *blizu* (near), *zraven* (next to), *pri* (near, at — static proximity)

> iz Maribora — from Maribor
> brez mleka — without milk
> do konca — until the end

**Prepositions governing the Dative:**
*k / h* (to, towards — persons), *kljub* (despite), *nasproti* (opposite, towards)

> Grem k zdravniku. — I am going to the doctor.
> Kljub dežju smo šli ven. — Despite the rain we went out.

**Prepositions governing the Accusative:**
*skozi* (through), *čez* (across, over), *v* (into — direction), *na* (onto — direction), *pod* (under — direction), *nad* (above — direction), *med* (between — direction), *pred* (in front of — direction), *za* (behind / for — direction)

> Grem v Maribor. — I am going to Maribor. (direction → accusative)
> Čez reko. — Across the river.

**Prepositions governing the Locative** (always with a preposition):
*v* (in — location), *na* (on — location), *pri* (at, near — location), *o* (about), *po* (around, according to), *ob* (by, next to)

> Sem v Mariboru. — I am in Maribor. (location → locative)
> Govorimo o politiki. — We are talking about politics.

**Prepositions governing the Instrumental** (always with a preposition):
*s / z* (with, together with), *med* (between — location), *pred* (in front of — location), *za* (behind — location), *nad* (above — location), *pod* (under — location)

> Grem s prijateljem. — I am going with a friend.
> Hiša stoji pred gozdom. — The house stands in front of the forest.

The alternation *s / z*: *s* is used before voiceless consonants and *z* before voiced consonants and vowels:
> *s prijateljem* (with a friend), *z bratom* (with a brother), *z Ano* (with Ana)

## Numbers and Numerals

### Cardinal Numbers (glavni števniki)

| Number | Slovenian | Number | Slovenian |
|--------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| 0 | nič | 11 | enajst |
| 1 | ena / en / eno | 12 | dvanajst |
| 2 | dve / dva | 13 | trinajst |
| 3 | tri | 20 | dvajset |
| 4 | štiri | 21 | enaindvajset |
| 5 | pet | 30 | trideset |
| 6 | šest | 40 | štirideset |
| 7 | sedem | 100 | sto |
| 8 | osem | 200 | dvesto |
| 9 | devet | 1000 | tisoč |
| 10 | deset | 1000000 | milijon |

The numerals **1** and **2** agree in gender: *en* (masc.), *ena* (fem.), *eno* (neut.); *dva* (masc./neut.), *dve* (fem.).

Numbers **1–4** require the noun in the nominative (1 = singular, 2 = dual, 3–4 = plural):
> *en mož* (one man), *dva moža* (two men), *trije možje* (three men), *štirje možje* (four men)

Numbers **5 and above** require the noun in the **genitive plural**:
> *pet mož* (five men), *sto mož* (a hundred men)

### Ordinal Numbers (vrstilni števniki)

Ordinal numbers are adjectives and fully declined: *prvi* (first), *drugi* (second), *tretji* (third), *četrti* (fourth), *peti* (fifth), *šesti* (sixth), *sedmi* (seventh), *osmi* (eighth), *deveti* (ninth), *deseti* (tenth), *enajsti* (eleventh), *dvajseti* (twentieth), *stoti* (hundredth), *tisočiti* (thousandth).

### Adverbial Numerals (prislovni števniki)

Adverbial numerals express how many times something happens and are invariable: *enkrat* (once), *dvakrat* (twice), *trikrat* (three times), *stokrat* (a hundred times).

## Sentence Structure

Slovenian's default word order is **Subject–Verb–Object (SVO)**, but the language is relatively free in its word order because case endings mark grammatical roles. Changing the order shifts **emphasis** rather than changing core meaning:

> Janez bere knjigo. — Janez reads the book. (neutral)
> Knjigo bere Janez. — It is Janez who reads the book. (Janez emphasized)
> Bere Janez knjigo? — Does Janez read the book? (question by intonation)

**Clitic second position (Wackernagel's Law)**: Short (clitic) forms of pronouns and the auxiliary verb *biti* must appear in the **second position** of a clause — after the first stressed word or phrase:

> *Janez mu je dal knjigo.* — Janez gave him the book. (clitics *mu je* after first word *Janez*)
> *Včeraj mu je dal knjigo.* — He gave him the book yesterday. (clitics after *včeraj*)

A sentence may lack an explicit predicate in colloquial or proverbial usage:
> *Rana ura, zlata ura.* — Early hour, golden hour. (The early bird catches the worm.)

**Negation**: *ne* is placed directly before the finite verb in the present or before the auxiliary in compound tenses:
> *Ne vem.* — I don't know.
> *Nisem videl.* — I didn't see.
> *Ne bom šel.* — I will not go.

Double negation with negative pronouns is obligatory (not a grammatical error):
> *Nihče ničesar ne ve.* — Nobody knows anything. (lit. Nobody knows nothing)

## Interjections

Interjections (medmeti) are uninflected words expressing emotions, greetings, imitations of sounds, or discourse markers. They can occasionally be nominalized (turned into nouns) and then decline regularly.

| Interjection | Usage / Meaning |
|-------------|-----------------|
| *uf* | Phew! / Gosh! |
| *ojoj* | Oh dear! / Ouch! |
| *brr* | Brr (cold)! |
| *oj / hej* | Hey! |
| *o ne* | Oh no! |
| *no* | Well (then) |
| *torej* | So, therefore |
| *šššš* | Shh! |
| *au* | Ouch! |
| *ha ha* | Ha ha (laughter) |

> Uf, končno smo na vrhu. — Phew, we're finally at the top.
> Oj, ti človek tam zadaj: kako ti je ime? — Hey, you person back there: what's your name?
> Brr, kako mraz je. — Brr, it's so cold.

Nominalized interjections decline as regular nouns:
> Ufov in ojojev se izogibajte, kajti bolnik je zelo ubog revež. — Steer clear of 'uf's and 'ojoj's, because the patient suffers greatly.

## Punctuation

Slovenian uses standard European punctuation. Notable conventions include:

- **Full stop (.)** ends declarative sentences. Also used after ordinal numbers written in digits: *1.* = *prvi* (first).
- **Comma (,)** separates clauses introduced by conjunctions (*da, ker, ko, če, ki* etc.) — a comma is **obligatory** before these subordinating conjunctions, unlike in English.
- **Question mark (?)** ends direct questions.
- **Exclamation mark (!)** ends imperatives and exclamations.
- **Quotation marks**: Slovenian uses **»guillemets«** (»besedilo«) or „low-high" style („besedilo"). In dialogue, a dash (—) introduces the speaker's words on a new line.
- **Dash (–)** is used for parenthetical remarks, in dialogue attribution, and for ranges.
- **Hyphen (-)** joins compound words or separates syllables.
- **Ellipsis (...)** marks trailing off or omitted text.

> »Dobro jutro,« je rekla Lojza. — "Good morning," said Aloysine.
> Lojza je rekla: »Dobro jutro.« — Aloysine said: "Good morning."
> — Dobro jutro. — "Good morning." (dialogue dash style)
